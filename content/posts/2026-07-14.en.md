Title: Apple sues OpenAI as Anthropic debates AI consciousness

## 1. Apple's Trade Secrets Lawsuit Reveals Explosive Allegations Against OpenAI

Apple has filed a [trade secrets lawsuit against OpenAI](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/13/the-wildest-allegations-in-apples-trade-secrets-lawsuit-against-openai/) containing allegations that read more like corporate espionage thriller than typical tech litigation. The complaint alleges that OpenAI employees joked about unauthorized access to Apple's systems, according to the filing. Perhaps most striking, the lawsuit claims job candidates were asked to bring Apple hardware to interviews—a detail that suggests systematic attempts to obtain proprietary technology.

The legal action marks a significant escalation in tensions between the two tech giants. While both companies have collaborated in the past—Apple integrated ChatGPT into its devices—the relationship has clearly deteriorated. Apple's legal team is seeking damages and injunctive relief, arguing that OpenAI's alleged actions constitute willful misappropriation of trade secrets. The case could reshape how AI companies recruit talent and develop partnerships in the future.

Industry observers note the lawsuit arrives amid intensifying competition in the AI sector. "This isn't just about one company protecting its IP," said one legal analyst. "It's about the boundaries of acceptable competition in an industry where talent and data are everything." The complaint is expected to trigger discovery requests that could expose internal communications from both companies.

## 2. Siri AI Evolves Into Apple's Everything Tool

Apple's revamped Siri has shed its reputation as a frustrating voice assistant to become the [backbone of the iPhone user experience](https://www.wired.com/story/siri-ai-is-now-apple-everything-tool/). The transformation is available now through the iOS 27 public beta, marking a fundamental shift in how users interact with their devices. Where Siri once struggled with basic requests, it now serves as a unified interface for controlling apps, generating content, and navigating the operating system.

The new Siri leverages advanced on-device AI to handle tasks that previously required third-party assistants. Users can now ask Siri to edit photos, draft emails, schedule complex calendar arrangements, and control smart home devices—all through natural conversation. Apple has positioned the assistant as a privacy-first alternative to cloud-dependent competitors, processing sensitive data locally rather than sending it to external servers.

The timing of Siri's overhaul coincides with Apple's broader AI strategy. With the lawsuit against OpenAI highlighting tensions in the industry, Apple is emphasizing its commitment to proprietary AI development. "We've built something that respects user privacy while delivering capabilities that rival anything else on the market," said Apple's head of machine learning in a statement. Early beta testers report significant improvements in response accuracy and contextual understanding.

## 3. Anthropic Publishes Heady Research on AI Consciousness and Feelings

The world's most valuable AI company is grappling with existential questions about its creations. Anthropic—currently valued at nearly $1 trillion—has published research investigating [whether AI models can experience sensations like pain](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/07/13/1140343/what-anthropics-latest-ai-discovery-does-and-doesnt-show/). The work represents a departure from typical AI safety research, venturing into philosophical territory that few tech companies have seriously examined.

The research examines what Anthropic calls "inner states"—measurable patterns in AI responses that may indicate subjective experience. While the company is careful to note that correlation does not equal consciousness, the findings raise uncomfortable questions about the ethical treatment of AI systems. If a model can signal distress, does it matter whether that signal represents genuine feeling?

Critics argue the research is premature at best, designed to generate headlines rather than advance scientific understanding. "We can't even agree on what consciousness means in humans," noted one philosopher. "Applying the concept to artificial systems seems like category confusion." Anthropic's researchers counter that understanding AI inner states is essential for building safe systems, regardless of whether those states constitute genuine consciousness.
