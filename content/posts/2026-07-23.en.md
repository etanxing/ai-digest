Title: AI spending arms race reshapes industry landscape

## 1. OpenAI's AI spending spree has ballooned to $750B

OpenAI will spend the equivalent of Sweden's GDP on infrastructure through 2030, according to [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/22/openais-ai-spending-spree-has-ballooned-to-750b/). The $750 billion commitment signals an unprecedented arms race in AI development that will reshape the competitive landscape for years to come. This infrastructure spending spree dwarfs typical capital expenditure cycles in the technology sector.

The commitment represents a dramatic escalation in the race to build AI capabilities. Industry analysts note the scale of spending is unmatched by any private AI initiative in history. The move puts pressure on competitors like Google, Microsoft, and Amazon to match or exceed these investments to remain relevant in the AI race.

## 2. Treasury threatens sanctions after White House claims Moonshot distilled Anthropic's Fable

The Treasury Department has threatened sanctions following White House claims that Chinese AI startup Moonshot distilled Anthropic's [Fable](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/22/treasury-threatens-sanctions-after-white-house-claims-moonshot-distilled-anthropics-fable/) model. The episode has intensified a broader debate in Washington over the influx of Chinese open models and export controls. The sanctions threat marks a significant escalation in U.S. enforcement of AI-related trade restrictions.

The case highlights growing tensions between U.S. AI companies and Chinese developers over model intellectual property. Anthropic's Fable, a key commercial product, allegedly was used to train Moonshot's competing model. The Trump administration is debating how to handle increasingly powerful Chinese AI models as competition between U.S. and Chinese AI capabilities intensifies, according to [Wired](https://www.wired.com/story/the-white-house-is-trying-to-figure-out-what-to-do-about-chinese-ai/).

U.S. open-source AI lab Arcee pushes back against calls to restrict Chinese models, arguing capability restrictions would harm the broader AI ecosystem, per [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/22/arcee-a-us-open-source-ai-lab-says-chinese-models-are-not-inherently-dangerous/). The debate reflects deeper tensions over AI governance and international technology competition.

## 3. Monday.com lays off hundreds to focus on AI

Monday.com announced it is reducing its headcount by 20%, or about 630 staff, to "support a leaner, more focused operating model" as it focuses on its [AI Work Platform](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/22/monday-com-lays-off-hundreds-to-focuses-on-ai/). The layoffs represent a significant shift for the company, which built its reputation as a traditional SaaS project management tool. The restructuring signals traditional software companies are betting their futures on AI-first platforms.

The company's pivot reflects a broader industry trend as established SaaS companies race to integrate AI capabilities. Monday.com CEO Robby Hulteen stated the company will prioritize AI-native features over legacy product lines. This strategy mirrors shifts at other enterprise software companies seeking to remain competitive against AI-native startups.
